[Andrewism's "We Need To Rethink Democracy"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=W5JEJ_L_Zjg) (June 2026) argues that democracy — even "real," direct, consensus-based democracy — is not a fellow traveller of anarchism but one of its obstacles. It's a substantial, well-sourced essay, and the argument is worth engaging with directly, including the parts that cut against how DOD itself talks about its work.

<!-- more -->

## The core argument

The video's claim, in short: *demos* + *kratos* means "rule of the people," and the "rule" part is not incidental. Etymology aside, the historical record of democracy — Athens, the American republic, every system that has called itself democratic since — has always meant binding collective decisions enforced on people who didn't consent to them, and a *demos* drawn narrower than everyone actually subject to that rule.

Several writers are cited to build the case:

- **Bob Black**, *[Debunking Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bob_Black)* (2011) — there is no self-evident, neutral set of voting rules (majority vs. plurality, quorums, who sets the agenda), and "preference is more or less, consent is yes or no" — majority rule overrides intensity of preference with raw headcount.
- **CrimethInc**, *[From Democracy to Freedom](https://crimethinc.com/2016/05/11/from-democracy-to-freedom-the-difference-between-rulership-and-self-determination)* (2016) — representative democracy is "the great achievement of two and a half centuries of democratic revolutions: instead of abolishing the means by which kings governed, they rendered those means popular."
- **David Graeber**, *[Fragments of an Anarchist Anthropology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fragments_of_an_Anarchist_Anthropology)* (2004) and *There Never Was a West* (2007) — credits stateless societies (Haudenosaunee, Amazigh, Sulawesi, Tallensi) with consensus-based collective decision-making predating and outside the Greek term, while still treating "democracy" broadly enough to include non-majoritarian consensus process.
- **Robert A. Dahl**, *[On Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On_Democracy)* (1998) — even sympathetic democratic theorists concede that direct democracy collides with basic arithmetic: at any scale, a handful of "fully participating members" become de facto representatives of everyone else, "yet nothing insures that the fully participating members are representative of the rest."
- Historical anarchists — Proudhon, Bakunin, Lucy Parsons — are quoted rejecting democracy (including direct and universal-suffrage forms) on the same grounds they rejected the state: "no man has any right to rule another man."

The video singles out **Murray Bookchin's** communalism as the clearest case of "anarchist democracy" actually being majoritarian rule by another name, and pushes back on contemporary anarchists (citing Lorenzo Kom'boa Ervin, Wayne Price, Kevin Carson) who use "democracy" to describe consensus-based, non-binding collective process. The argument isn't just etymological pedantry: it's that *calling* consensus process "democracy" imports the connotation of binding, enforced collective will — and risks reproducing it in practice. "What begins as a compromise in language risks becoming a compromise in practice."

The proposed alternative is anarchy: no demos (no boundary of who counts), no kratos (no enforced collective decision), free association as the organising principle, and agreements that bind only as long as participants freely affirm them.

## Where this lands relative to DOD

DOD's [Philosophy](../../philosophy/index.md) page is explicitly **agnostic about which democratic model is correct** — it doesn't take a position on direct vs. representative vs. sortition-based vs. liquid democracy, and treats "democracy" as a broad, contested family of governance experiments rather than one fixed institution. This critique sits one level up from that agnosticism: it isn't arguing for a *better* democratic model, it's arguing that the category itself — any system that produces collectively binding decisions enforced on dissenters — is the thing worth rejecting.

That's a genuinely different claim than anything DOD's existing concept pages cover. [`direct-democracy`](../../concepts/direct-democracy.md), [`deliberative-democracy`](../../concepts/deliberative-democracy.md), and [`democratic-confederalism`](../../concepts/democratic-confederalism.md) all describe ways of organising binding collective decision-making. The video's argument is that *binding* is the load-bearing problem, independent of how participatory or consensus-driven the process getting there is.

A few of the critique's sharper points are hard to wave away even from a pro-democracy vantage:

- **The majority/minority framing is itself manufactured.** Voting compresses individual, varying-intensity preferences into a binary bloc structure that didn't exist before the vote was taken. This is a real cost of any majoritarian mechanism, not just a rhetorical flourish.
- **Direct democracy doesn't escape representation, it just hides it.** Dahl's point — that participation time limits mean a small subset of "fully participating members" end up speaking for everyone else, with no guarantee they're representative — describes a failure mode that shows up in real assemblies, not just theoretical ones.
- **Voting-theory results (Condorcet cycling, the McKelvey-Schofield chaos theorem) are a genuine practical problem**, not an anarchist talking point — they're standard results in social choice theory that mean majority-rule outcomes in multidimensional policy spaces can be manipulated via agenda control to produce nearly any result.

Where the critique is more contestable: it treats "binding decision enforced on non-consenters" as inherently illegitimate regardless of how the decision was reached or what it's protecting (the video doesn't address, for instance, how an alegal society resolves a case where one party's free association directly harms another who hasn't consented to that harm — only that "best practice will entail consultation and negotiation"). That's the standard anarchist response to the standard objection, and it's an old argument; this post isn't the place to adjudicate it, but a human author publishing this should decide how much space to give that tension.

## Why this might be worth a DOD post at all

DOD monitors organisations and concepts "regardless of ideological label," per the curation standard on the [Organisations](../../organisations/organisations.md) page — anarchist and anarchist-adjacent governance experiments (consensus process, sociocracy, free association models) are in scope on the same basis as electoral reform groups, provided they're not purely abuse-documentation without governance engagement. A critique this substantive, aimed squarely at the category DOD is named after, is the kind of thing the landscape should be able to hold without flinching — and a published post is one place to do that holding visibly, rather than only in private discussion.
