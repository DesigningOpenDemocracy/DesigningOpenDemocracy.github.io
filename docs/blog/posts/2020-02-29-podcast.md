This bonus episode was recorded as a free-flowing warm-up conversation with Ben Ballingall of the Flux Party — released after [the main 11 February 2020 conversation](2020-02-13-podcast.md), as additional material exploring how the Flux system itself actually works.

## From idea to ballot: Flux's founding story

Brian opens by asking about Flux's history. Ben traces it to the middle of 2015, when **Max Kaye** and **Nathan Spataro** — the party's co-founders — came together around a shared idea for a new form of democracy. What set them apart, in Ben's telling, wasn't just the idea but the ability to build it:

> "They had both an idea for this new form of democracy. But, perhaps even more importantly, they had the ability — and the code — to actually develop it."
> — Ben Ballingall

They built a prototype app and used it to generate enough momentum to get the party formally registered ahead of the 2016 federal election — by Ben's account, "late 2015 would have been the true state of Flux, just the two of them." For the deeper theoretical case Kaye and Spataro built on top of that prototype — selectorate theory, Popperian fallibilism, and the critique of static majoritarianism — see the [Flux Party page](../../organisations/flux-party.md) on the Democracy Landscape.

## The pitch, and what stays the same

Asked what Flux actually is, Ben gives the elevator pitch:

> "We're a political party registered in Australia... that believes in, or proposes, issue-based direct democracy. That's a way for all the citizenry to vote on the issues that come up in Parliament, and have those results aggregated and shown to a representative — and the representative will do what their community votes for in Parliament."
> — Ben Ballingall

Brian's gloss — a system "where people get to do a bit more direct democracy in their government" — gets a blunt confirmation: "it's a very straight up and down democracy, the people voting on every single issue."

One thing IBDD does *not* change is the three-year electoral cycle for choosing your MP. What changes is what that MP does once elected: instead of voting on their own judgement or party line, a Flux MP votes according to the aggregated preferences of their electorate's app users, issue by issue. The mechanism is contingent on what's already put before Parliament — it reroutes who controls each vote within the existing process rather than replacing the process itself.

## The cultural half of the project

The conversation's most interesting turn is Ben's framing of Flux as *one component* of a larger picture — and his use of the phrase "designing an open democracy" to describe that larger picture, distinct from Flux's own mechanism:

> "I'd say Flux is one element — if I can — of designing an open democracy. Flux posits a true democratic form: it allows everybody to vote on issues. Right now, it's contingent on Parliament — on what is put up in Parliament primarily."
> — Ben Ballingall

The missing piece, in his account, is cultural rather than technical: how do people get *informed* about the issues they're suddenly empowered to vote on? Brian pushes on this directly — isn't there a risk that a voting mechanism alone doesn't address media or cultural representation? Ben agrees, and reaches for what he calls "the million, billion, trillion dollar question":

> "When we think of voting, especially nowadays, we think of it as fighting for a team — this tribal team that's always held this ideology, that comes with these fifty points attached. Really changing how culture looks at politics, issue by issue, and how those issues actually relate individually — that's a huge cultural shift."
> — Ben Ballingall

His example is a thought experiment about cross-party voting: today, it would be "completely absurd" for someone to publicly back one Greens initiative and a different One Nation initiative in the same week — even if, on the merits, the Greens policy is a sound environmental measure and the One Nation policy is a sound piece of rural infrastructure spending. "They can both be right," Ben says, "and in today's politics... that's completely absurd." IBDD's vote-by-vote structure makes that kind of split judgement *mechanically* possible; whether voters are *culturally* ready to make it is, on Ben's account, a separate and much harder problem — one that has to "grow... in tandem with the ability to vote on issues and control your MP."

## A walkthrough of the citizen app

Asked for a concrete walkthrough — "how would the ordinary voter in the public participate in your system?" — Ben sketches the user journey end to end, with one upfront condition: a Flux MP has to have already been elected in your electorate for any of this to matter. From there:

1. An issue comes up — Ben's example is scrolling Facebook and seeing a bill being debated.
2. You download the Flux app (or visit the website) and create an account, logging in with your federal electoral roll details — verified by the government as proof you're a real, single person.
3. The app shows your state and federal upcoming bills with a countdown — "this is going to be voted on in four days... this is going to be voted on in three days."
4. Tapping a bill gives a synopsis, links to the proposing party's information, and — where available — counter-information from other parties. Flux deliberately stays out of this layer: "this is definitely a level where we don't want to be proposing any information. So you won't find information from Flux."
5. You vote yes, no, or choose not to vote.
6. You're shown the aggregated result for your electorate, with a one-tap option to post to Twitter, post to Facebook, or email your representative directly.

The design intent is that someone who feels strongly about a single bill can go from noticing it on social media to recording a vote *and* contacting their MP within the same few taps.

## Trust, blockchain, and the Iowa caucus problem

Brian raises the obvious objection: a system this dependent on smartphones and technology invites public anxiety about security, especially after high-profile failures. He brings up the Iowa caucus app fiasco from earlier in 2020 — a vote-reporting bug that delayed results for days and became a national story about whether electronic vote-counting could be trusted.

Ben's response separates the *technical* failure from how it gets *perceived*:

> "That exact fault was an actual fault in the code — it had been coded poorly — but in people's psyche it'll just be: an app broke and it screwed up the vote. Tech gets reduced down so quickly."
> — Ben Ballingall

His case for Flux's own approach rests on three claims: the ledger is blockchain-based and therefore immutable; the system uses "the strongest security and anonymisation in the industry — the same stuff that protects your banking and online communication"; and the code is open source, so "if it's got a flaw, people will find it. And right now we are telling you there are no flaws."

He's candid that public trust in blockchain has been a moving target. Three years into Flux, he says, "how much we have to explain blockchain now, compared to three years ago, is markedly less" — most people now have at least a rough association between blockchain/crypto and secure, anonymous storage of value, which makes the leap to "so you can see how that could be used for voting" much shorter than it used to be. His closing line on the topic is unapologetically blunt: "it works, and you can't prove us wrong — and the people that can prove us wrong agree with us."
